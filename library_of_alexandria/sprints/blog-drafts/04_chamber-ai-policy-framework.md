A Chamber of Commerce should create an AI policy by defining five things: what AI tools the Chamber uses and for which functions, what data the Chamber provides to those tools, how member and donor information is protected, what oversight and review cadence the Board enforces, and what the Chamber discloses publicly about its AI use. ## H2 — Why Chamber AI policy is a Board-level issue (not a staff-level one)

AI policy is not IT policy. It's governance. Three reasons:

**Fiduciary exposure.** When a Chamber uses AI tools, someone is making choices about what data flows where. If member contact information or donor records end up in training sets, retention logs, or third-party systems without consent, the Chamber is exposed. The Board — not staff — holds fiduciary responsibility for that exposure.

**Reputational risk.** A Chamber that deploys AI-generated content without disclosure, or that uses AI to automate member communications without clarity, creates a trust problem if members find out through discovery rather than announcement. Disclosure is a Board-level decision.

**Legal evolution.** State and federal AI regulations are shifting quickly. The Board needs to be able to demonstrate it considered compliance at the policy level — not delegated it to an IT contractor. "We weren't aware" is not a defense.

Staff executes the policy. The Board sets it.

## H2 — The five-element framework

### Element 1 — What AI tools, for which functions

The policy enumerates which AI systems the Chamber uses and the specific function each performs. Not brand names (those change); categories.

Example language:
*"The Chamber uses AI systems for: (a) member marketing content generation, (b) member communication drafting, (c) meeting minute transcription and summarization, (d) document review and research, (e) calendar and event scheduling assistance. New categories of AI usage require Board ratification before deployment."*

This element prevents scope creep and gives the Board a defined surface to govern.

### Element 2 — What data is shared with AI systems

The policy specifies which data categories can flow to AI systems and which cannot.

- **Permitted:** public information, published meeting minutes, Chamber marketing copy drafts
- **Restricted (requires Board approval):** member contact information, donor records, financial data
- **Prohibited:** Social Security numbers, credit card data, legally protected communications

Example language:
*"Chamber staff may use approved AI systems with public information and Chamber operational documents. Sharing member personally-identifiable information, donor records, or financial data with AI systems requires explicit Board approval."*

### Element 3 — How member and donor information is protected

This element specifies data handling even when AI use is permitted.

- Data provided to AI systems is not used for model training (contract-level vendor assurance)
- Data retention in AI logs is minimized and deleted on Chamber's regular retention schedule
- Staff training on what to avoid sharing

### Element 4 — Oversight and review cadence

The Board enforces the policy through a regular review cycle.

- Quarterly Board review of AI usage categories and incidents
- Annual policy update
- Ad-hoc review triggered by new tool adoption or regulatory change

### Element 5 — Public disclosure

The policy specifies what the Chamber discloses to members about AI use.

- Annual disclosure in member materials stating the Chamber uses AI for specified categories of work
- Clear labeling of substantially AI-drafted member communications
- Opt-out mechanism for members who prefer human-only correspondence in specific contexts

## H2 — The two most common policy mistakes (and the fixes)

**Mistake 1: Defining AI by brand name instead of function category.**
Brand names change faster than Board meetings happen. A policy that permits "tool X" becomes meaningless when tool X is replaced by tool Y. Fix: define by function category (content generation, scheduling assistance, transcription) and let staff select specific vendors within approved categories.

**Mistake 2: Missing the disclosure element entirely.**
Most draft Chamber AI policies cover internal use but forget public disclosure. If members discover AI use through rumor rather than disclosure, trust erodes. Fix: include a one-sentence annual disclosure in member renewal materials and clear labeling on substantially AI-drafted external communications.

Both fixes take under an hour of Board time to close.

## H2 — Board approval process: from draft to vote in 45 days

**Week 1:** Executive Director drafts policy using the [AMG template →](/resources/chamber-ai-policy-template).

**Week 2:** Committee review (Governance Committee, if Chamber has one; otherwise two Board officers).

**Week 3:** Feedback incorporated; legal counsel review (if applicable).

**Week 4–5:** Full Board discussion at regular meeting. Policy adopted by motion.

**Week 6:** Policy posted internally; staff training session; public disclosure copy added to next member newsletter.

45 days from draft to fully operational. Faster if the Board meets more frequently.

## H2 — How to integrate AI policy with existing Chamber bylaws and risk management

AI policy typically sits as a standalone policy document cross-referenced from the Chamber's:

- **Bylaws** (general reference in the governance section)
- **Employee handbook** (AI tool usage expectations)
- **Data privacy policy** (cross-reference to specific handling rules)
- **Member agreement** (disclosure language)

The AMG template includes cross-reference language for each of these. The Chamber's attorney reviews; most Chambers ship the template with minor edits.

## H2 — FAQ

**Q: Does every Chamber need a formal AI policy even if they don't actively use AI?**
Yes — because the policy defines what's *not* permitted even before staff starts experimenting. A pre-adoption policy prevents the "we accidentally used an AI tool that retained our data" scenario.

**Q: Is the AMG Chamber AI Policy Template legally binding on adoption?**
On Board adoption it becomes the Chamber's internal governance. It's not a contract with any external party. Every Chamber should have its attorney review before vote.

**Q: What if we're already using AI informally?**
That's the most common starting state. The policy retroactively formalizes what's in use, identifies gaps, and puts guardrails in place. Most Chambers adopt in 45 days from draft to vote.

**Q: Does the policy need Board approval annually?**
Annual review is best practice. Full re-approval isn't required unless substantive changes are made.

**Q: Where does this policy sit relative to other Chamber policies?**
Standalone document cross-referenced from Bylaws, Employee Handbook, Data Privacy Policy, and Member Agreement. The AMG template includes cross-reference language for each.
